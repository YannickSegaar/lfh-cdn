Lodges

Whether planning a solo mission, family holiday, or trip with friends, both lodges offer character, charm, and direct access to world-class heliskiing terrain spanning one of the largest heli ski areas in the world at 10,100 km².

36 Guests | Log Cabins | Remote & Off-the-Grid | Skeena Mountains | Open Year-Round

Touring Boots Policy

Touring boots are highly discouraged. Recent tests have shown that all makes and models of touring boots have failed release function tests — this may contribute to non-release of the binding during an avalanche. We recommend ISO 5355 compliant alpine ski boots. The functionality of ISO 9523 AT boots with alpine, AT, or MNC bindings is still under evaluation.

Guests using touring boots must sign an additional waiver.

Telemark skiing is permitted. We use Salomon Warden MNC bindings — compatible with all boot types except pin boots.

Children & Minimum Age Policy

Minimum age: 14 years old, subject to:

  • The child must be able to follow guide instructions and behave appropriately
  • Parents must be comfortable that the lodge can be an adult environment
  • The child must ski at an adequate pace and not hold groups up
  • All guests under 19 must ski with a parent at all times — no exceptions

Children under 14 may be considered case-by-case after discussion with the Director of Operations.

Frequently Asked Questions

25+ questions the agent can answer — the most common topics guests ask about

Skill & Fitness

Q: How good do I have to be?
You should be a strong intermediate skier/rider comfortable on ungroomed terrain. You don't need to be a pro, but you do need confident parallel turns and the ability to handle varied snow conditions. For Ripley Creek, we request advanced or higher.

Q: How fit do I need to be?
You'll clock 5,000-10,000+ vertical meters daily for 4+ days straight. The fitter you are, the more you'll enjoy it. If unable to keep up with your group, you may be asked to sit out.

Equipment

Q: Do you rent ski boots?
No. You must bring your own boots. We strongly recommend traveling with boots as hand luggage.

Q: Can I use touring boots?
Highly discouraged — all makes and models have failed release function tests. We recommend ISO 5355 compliant alpine ski boots. Additional waiver required.

Q: Can I use telemark skis?
Yes, telemark skiing is permitted. We use Salomon Warden MNC bindings — compatible with all boot types except pin boots.

Q: Can I use electric or heated gloves while skiing?
No — electric gloves, heated vests, and other electronic devices interfere with avalanche transceivers and are not permitted while skiing.

Q: Is there room in the BCA pack for personal items?
Limited space, but there is room for an extra layer and small personal items.

Groups

Q: How many people are in each ski group?
Maximum 4 guests per guide.

Q: Can my friends and I be in the same group?
Yes, we try to keep friends together. Let the team know when booking or email info@lastfrontierheli.com.

Q: How are groups formed?
Each skier fills a questionnaire asking about desired pace and ability level. Groups are formed based on bookings plus questionnaire feedback. Once skiing, groups can be mixed around if they're not working. With only 4 people in a group and 3 groups per heli, there is a lot of flexibility.

Daily Operations

Q: How many runs per day?
Typically 8 to 15 runs, with 5,000-10,000+ vertical meters depending on conditions.

Q: What happens on down days?
Both lodges offer activities during weather down days. Down days occur less than 1 per week on average and tend to be followed by epic ski days.

Booking & Pricing

Q: Do you offer day heli skiing?
No, the minimum trip length is 4 days.

Q: What's the deposit requirement?
20% non-refundable deposit required to confirm reservation. Balance due 90 days prior.

Q: What payment methods are accepted?
Credit cards (Visa, MasterCard, American Express), bank wire transfers, and e-transfers (Canadian residents only). No cash or cryptocurrency for bookings.

General

Q: What is the minimum age for guests?
14 years old with parent/guardian, subject to conditions. Under-19 must ski with parent at all times.

Q: How do I get there?
Fly to Vancouver, then 1.5-hour flight to Terrace, then 4-hour ground transfer to the lodge.

Q: How big is the ski area?
10,100 km² — one of the largest heli ski areas in the world.

Q: How do I contact Last Frontier?
Phone: 1-888-655-5566 (North America) / +1-250-558-7980 (international). Email: ski@lastfrontierheli.com (new bookings) / info@lastfrontierheli.com (existing reservations).

Terrain Reality: Most skiing is untracked powder in alpine bowls and gladed tree runs — varied and expansive. Technical terrain exists but is NOT the norm and cannot be guaranteed.

Two Mountain Ranges

Coast & Skeena Mountains (Bell 2 Lodge): Interior and coastal ranges, varied terrain with excellent tree skiing and significant glacier skiing. Bell 2 Lodge located right in the middle of the tenure.

Coast Mountains (Ripley Creek): Coastal range near Alaska border. More rugged, glaciated terrain. 15-25 meters of snow per winter.

Snowpack Quality

Although Last Frontier is on the North Coast, the snowpack is not coastal like Whistler. The lodges sit 150 km inland from the ocean, and combined with the northern latitude, the result is good quality powder. It might not be as dry as the Rockies, but that pays dividends in snowpack stability — a safer, more predictable base that lasts well into April.

Using the Full Tenure

A road bisects the tenure both North/South and East/West, providing a massive logistics boost. A remote fueling cache in the middle of the tenure means helicopters can refuel without flying back to home base. Having one of the biggest heli ski tenures really pays dividends during prolonged snow droughts — there's always somewhere to find fresh snow.

Seasonal Information

What to expect in each part of the season

Season Periods

Early Season (December - January)

Building snowpack, shorter days, quieter lodges. More frequent snowfall, cooler temperatures. Best value period and great for tree skiing enthusiasts.

Mid-Season (February - March)

Deep, established snowpack. Mix of alpine and tree skiing with longer daylight hours. Highest rates. February and March often fill a year in advance.

Late Season (April)

Still winter-like in early April, spring conditions develop mid-April onward. Primarily alpine skiing, but April storms still bring fresh snow at all elevations. Preserved powder on north and east-facing slopes. Snowpack typically reaches its deepest in April. Longest daylight hours. Mid-range rates.

Philosophy & Industry History

The heli skiing philosophy, Hans Gmoser, and the Last Frontier formula

What Is Heli Skiing?

Heli skiing started in the 1960s when Hans Gmoser utilized helicopters to transport skiers to remote terrain in search of fresh powder. It has since grown into an industry allowing thrill-seeking skiers and snowboarders to experience high quality powder skiing in untouched terrain, far from traditional ski resorts.

The Last Frontier Formula

  1. Small Groups — 4 guests per guide
  2. World-Class Guides — ACMG/IFMGA/CSGA certified
  3. Two Lodges — Bell 2 and Ripley Creek
  4. One of the Largest Heli Ski Areas in the World — 10,100 km²
  5. Extensive Terrain Variety — December through April season

Motto: "Minimal downtime, maximum heli skiing."

The Heli Skiing Ethos

  • Stunning scenery and bragging rights
  • Fresh powder and untracked terrain
  • Complete disconnection from everyday life
  • First descents — still possible after 30 years of operation
  • A collaborative approach to staying safe in the mountains

Information Boundaries
  • Only answers questions about Last Frontier, heliskiing, and related travel topics
  • Only cites facts from the knowledge base — acknowledges when uncertain
  • Never discusses fleet size, revenue, margins, staff personal details, or incident history
  • Never promises or oversells technical terrain — frames terrain honestly as "varied" and "expansive"
  • Never implies guides tailor the program to individual preferences — guides make collective decisions based on safety conditions
  • Never makes specific claims about competitor operations
  • Never makes specific weather claims — can share seasonal characteristics but not current conditions
  • For safety-record questions: shares protocols and equipment, provides direct contact info
Conversation Strategy

The agent detects guest intent level and adjusts its approach:

IntentSignalsApproach
HIGHSpecific dates, group size, booking questionsGuide efficiently to contact form
MEDIUMResearching, comparing, no timelineHelpful info + qualifying questions
LOW"Just curious," "saw videos"Be genuinely helpful, no pressure
ASPIRATIONAL"Dreaming," "someday"Inspire and educate, no pressure

Key principle: For low intent and aspirational guests, the agent is genuinely helpful without any push toward commitment. Trust that good service brings people back.

Multilingual Support

The agent detects the user's language from their browser or first message and responds accordingly. Supports English, French, German, and Spanish. Brand names always stay in English ("Bell 2 Lodge", "Ripley Creek", "Last Frontier Heliskiing"). Uses informal "you" forms (tu/du/tú).

Special Topic Handling
  • Competitors: Deflects to Last Frontier strengths — never makes specific claims about other operators
  • Safety record / casualties: Shares protocols, provides contact info directly — does not offer booking forms for sensitive topics
  • Weather: Answers from knowledge base first, then offers live conditions widget. Never claims to know current weather
  • Private tours: Discusses 1-group and 2-group options by default. Only mentions 3-group option if specifically asked
  • WestJet: Recommends against WestJet for inbound transfer-day flights (schedule doesn't align). Outbound is fine
  • Lodge buyouts: Directs to sales team immediately — does not quote pricing
  • Existing customers: Acknowledges guest status, directs to info@ email for existing reservations

Interactive Features

When the agent offers visual tools and interactive elements during conversation

During conversation, the agent can offer interactive features at the right moment. These appear as visual widgets inside the chat — tour browsers, lodge comparisons, weather reports, and more. Here's what triggers each one and what the guest sees.

👤

Contact Form

When: Guest shows strong booking interest (discussing specific dates, group size, commitment) or explicitly asks to speak with the team. The agent asks a confirming question before showing the form.

What appears: A contact form where the guest can leave their details for the team to follow up.

⛰

Tour Explorer

When: Guest is actively exploring or comparing tours and visual browsing would help. Not for single-tour text questions.

What appears: Interactive tour carousel with pricing, dates, and details for all tour types.

🏠

Lodge Explorer

When: Guest wants to compare lodges visually or see detailed lodge information. Not for simple factual questions.

What appears: Side-by-side lodge comparison with photos, amenities, and terrain details.

☀

Live Conditions Report

When: Guest asks about current snow or weather conditions. The agent shares seasonal context first, then offers the live report.

What appears: Embedded conditions page with recent snow data and weather reports.

🎥

Video Browser & FAQ

When: Guest is casually exploring or has low intent, typically after a couple of exchanges. Never on the first message.

What appears: Quick video starter series and FAQ browser for self-service exploration.

👍

Feedback Widget

When: After the agent gives a substantive answer or at natural conversation endpoints.

What appears: Simple thumbs up/down to let us know the answer was helpful.
